Authentic Brands Group, a brand licensing company whose portfolio includes the Marilyn Monroe estate, acquired the Prince name out of that bankruptcy and set up three regional operating partners to run the brand globally. Prince racquets still exist and the Phantom line still earns genuine praise from independent reviewers, but the engineering culture that produced those breakthroughs, the people, the decisions, the obsession with a single sport, no longer exists in the form it once did. What you are buying today is a licensed version of a name that used to belong to something else entirely. The name on the frame is the same. The company behind it is not.
Five brands, five reveals, and now the one that looked at all of this and never went down that road.
Babolat was founded in Lyon, France in 1875, is run today by Eric Babolat, the great-great-grandson of the founder, and has never been acquired, taken private, or absorbed into a larger group. No conglomerate, no fashion house, no private equity firm, no brand licensing company has ever owned it. When Eric's father Pierre died in 1998, Eric took over the business at twenty-eight years old and kept it in the family. String production remains in France, and every decision about what goes into a Babolat product is made by people whose only industry is tennis. That is a structural fact that separates them from every other brand on this list, and it is not something that shows up on the hang tag.
Nadal began his relationship with Babolat at age nine, and Alcaraz and Holger Rune are with the brand today. The Pure Drive and Pure Aero are among the most widely used recreational racquets in the world, and the brand carries genuine presence at Roland Garros and Wimbledon. Independence does not mean cheap, because the Pure Aero is a premium product at a premium price, but the people deciding what goes into a Babolat racquet have no other industry to answer to and no unrelated revenue targets to protect.
Wilson owns your racquet and your strings. Head owns your tour frame and the ball you never thought to question. Lacoste owns the brand you chose to escape fashion marketing. Frasers Group owns the ball that has been at Wimbledon for over a century. And one French family still owns the only brand on this list that was never sold. Knowing who owns what does not mean the gear is bad. But ownership tells you whose priorities drive the product. Most players never think to look any of this up. Now you have.
